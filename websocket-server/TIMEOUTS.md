# Timeout Management in `websocket-server`

The `websocket-server` utilizes several timers, primarily within `ari-client.ts`, to manage call flow, handle user interaction, and prevent calls from getting stuck. These timers are configurable via environment variables, which override the default values specified in `config/default.json`. Understanding these timers is crucial for fine-tuning the application's behavior for different operational scenarios and network conditions.

## Timer Overview

Timers control various stages of a call, including:
*   Initial connection and greeting phases.
*   Waiting for speech to begin after activation.
*   Detecting the end of user speech.
*   Managing overall call duration.
*   Handling DTMF input sequences.

Most timers, upon expiring, will trigger a cleanup of the call resources, potentially including hanging up the call, to prevent indefinite resource usage.

“We are witnessing a shift from what was once the last bastion of the wild to the new Jackson Hole, where the local feeling of this unique place is being lost. I believe in property rights, but the proposed development would push us over the edge.” — longtime Wilson resident Doug Kyle.

The proposed private club development known as the Jackson Hole Golf & Tennis Club, or the “Northworks” parcel, has become a lightning rod here for the fight to protect the rural character of Teton County’s northwest corner.

This plan for a 50-acre expansion of the existing resort, which would include 170 residential units, a private club with pools, a golf course, spa, gym, tennis courts, restaurants and a new irrigation system, is a hot topic in Jackson Hole.

The Teton County Board of County Commissioners should be commended for listening to the public and denying the developer’s initial request for amendments to the Teton County Comprehensive Plan. This is a good start. But now the community needs to follow up on this decision by demanding that our elected officials strengthen the Comprehensive Plan and the county’s Land Development Regulations (LDRs) to prevent this sort of development from happening again.

The current Teton County Comprehensive Plan has a provision in it to protect rural character that is directly applicable to the Northworks parcel: Section 1.5, which states, “Protect natural resources, working lands, and scenic values on private property by limiting land development.”

This is a critical section to address the preservation of our rural character, but it is not strong enough. The current LDRs and Comprehensive Plan give too much room for interpretation. For example, the definition of “working lands” is not defined in the LDRs. This omission has allowed developers to argue that since the Northworks parcel is not currently being used for commercial agricultural purposes, it should be treated as other private land. This reasoning ignores the fact that the land has a long history of agricultural use and is part of a larger agricultural landscape.

The LDRs need to be strengthened to protect the rural character of Teton County. This means defining “working lands” more broadly to include land that has been used for agricultural purposes in the past, even if it is not currently in production. It also means limiting the amount of development that can occur on these lands.

I have lived in Teton County for more than 30 years. I have witnessed the changes that have occurred as the county has become more popular and developed. I understand that change is inevitable, but I also believe that it is important to protect what makes Teton County special.

The Northworks parcel is a prime example of the type of land that needs to be protected. It is a beautiful piece of property with a long history of agricultural use. It is also home to a variety of wildlife, including elk, deer, and moose.

The proposed development would destroy this rural character. It would also put a strain on our already overburdened infrastructure, including our roads, water, and sewer systems.

We need to send a clear message to our elected officials that we want to protect the rural character of Teton County. We need to tell them that we do not want to see our county become another Aspen or Vail.

We can do this by supporting the Teton County Board of County Commissioners in their decision to deny the developer’s request for amendments to the Teton County Comprehensive Plan. We can also do this by demanding that our elected officials strengthen the Comprehensive Plan and the LDRs to prevent this sort of development from happening again.

Let’s work together to protect what makes Teton County special. Let’s make sure that future generations can enjoy the same rural character that we do today.
